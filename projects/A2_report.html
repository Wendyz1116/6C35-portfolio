Subtheme: Zoning and Housing Supply

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. Do areas with a higher percentage of single-family homes tend to have more older or retired people?

    2. Motivation: Growing up in Florida, I always heard people say that it’s a good place to retire to since there is usually more space and larger homes for families. This motivated me to look into whether this is common in Massachusetts, too where people other than 65 tend to live in areas with a higher percentage of single-family home zoning. I feel like this could be the case since older people may have more money to afford single-family homes and they may want more space for their families.

  2. Do people of color or people with low incomes live in areas with a lower percentage of single-family homes?

    3. In “Houses are too expensive. Apartments are too small. Is this a fix?” (Washington Post) they said that “zoning laws [were] an implicit strategy to price people of color and low-income people out of certain neighborhoods.” This makes me wonder if neighborhoods with a higher percentage of single-family housing end up having fewer people of color and people with lower incomes. I feel like this could be the case since single-family homes are more expensive and people may not be able to afford them, so they may end up living in areas with more cheaper multi-family housing options which may have a lower percentage of single-family homes.

  3. Do areas with a lower percentage of single-family homes have a higher population?

    4. In “Houses are too expensive. Apartments are too small. Is this a fix?” (Washington Post), they claim that with fewer single-family-only zones, there can be more housing options for people. This makes me wonder if there is a correlation between the percentage of single-family housing and the number of people living there. I feel like there could be a correlation since areas with more single-family homes may have fewer people living there since they are more expensive and there is less options.

Discoveries & Insights

Summary

In general, I think this dataset allowed for great initial exploration of how the percentage of single-family housing can affect the age, race, income, and population count of the people living there. However, I feel like there are some errors in data entry, specifically in the case where the number of households earning under $10,000 is higher than the total population. I also think land size is an important context that can be added, since it affects how many people an area can actually support.

More specifically, I learned that people over 65 generally do live in areas with a higher percentage of single-family housing, which aligns with my experiences growing up in Florida, where many retired people tend to live in larger, single-family homes. It was interesting to look more closely at cities like Boston, Medford, and Quincy to better understand why their values fall outside the general trend, though. I was surprised that there was not a clearer trend showing a higher number of people of color or lower-income residents in areas with less single-family housing. I think this could be explored further by comparing the number of people earning different income levels across varying percentages of single-family housing.

It is also surprising that areas with larger populations generally have higher percentages of single-family homes. These realizations seem to counter the ideas supported by the readings. It was also interesting to see that whether a place is or is not an MBTA community does not really seem to strongly affect the number of people living there.
